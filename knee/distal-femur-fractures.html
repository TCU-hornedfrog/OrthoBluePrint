Distal Femur Fractures

Fractures involving the femur from the metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction down to the articular surface of the femoral condyles.
OTA Code: 33 Bimodal Distribution 3‚Äì6% of Femur Fractures

Key concept: If it involves the joint ‚Üí it matters surgically. Articular surface separated from shaft requires anatomic reduction.

Epidemiology

Population 1
Young Males
High-energy trauma mechanism. Fractures tend to be displaced and comminuted due to force magnitude.
Population 2
Elderly Osteoporotic Females
Low-energy fall from standing. Bone quality is the limiting factor ‚Äî think osteoporosis immediately.

Elderly + low energy mechanism ‚Üí always consider poor bone quality. This drives implant selection (locking plates, arthroplasty).

OTA Classification

A
Extra-Articular
Fracture does not involve the articular surface. Metaphyseal and diaphyseal patterns.
B
Partial Articular
Part of the joint surface is still attached to the shaft.
33-B3 = Hoffa Fracture (coronal plane split of a condyle)
C
Complete Articular
Joint completely separated from shaft. Restore articular surface before fixation to shaft.
04

Presentation & Must-Not-Miss

Pain, inability to weight bear, swelling, and visible deformity are expected. Always inspect for open fracture (skin tenting, wounds around the knee).

üö®
MUST NOT MISS: Vascular Injury
The popliteal artery is at risk. Check pulses bilaterally. ABI <0.9 ‚Üí angiography indicated immediately.
05

Imaging

X-rays: AP + lateral of the knee. Image the entire femur to rule out ipsilateral fractures. Look carefully for intra-articular extension on every view.

ü©ª
Hoffa Fracture Pearl: Coronal plane fracture most commonly of the lateral condyle. Seen on the lateral view ‚Äî frequently missed on AP. Any articular irregularity ‚Üí get CT.

CT Scan: Indicated for any suspected intra-articular fracture and mandatory for preoperative planning.

06

Treatment

üìå
Most distal femur fractures are operative. Nonoperative reserved for nondisplaced stable fractures or nonambulatory patients.
01
ORIF ‚Äî Locking Plate
Most common for displaced and intra-articular fractures
‚ñº
Indications
  • Displaced fractures
  • Intra-articular fractures (33-B, 33-C)
  • Metaphyseal comminution
Key Points
  • Restore articular surface first, then fix to shaft
  • Locking plates control coronal alignment
  • Minimize soft tissue stripping ‚Äî consider MIPO technique
Articular reduction must be anatomic. Address the joint before the shaft.
02
Retrograde Intramedullary Nail
Extra-articular and simple intra-articular patterns
‚ñº
Indications
  • Extra-articular fractures (OTA-33A)
  • Some simple intra-articular patterns
Key Points
  • Starting point: center of intercondylar notch on AP, Blumensaat's line on lateral
  • Better load sharing than plate ‚Äî good union rates
  • Risk: Malalignment, especially recurvatum
Start point is everything ‚Äî error leads to coronal or sagittal malalignment.
03
Distal Femoral Replacement
Elderly with poor bone stock or periprosthetic fracture
‚ñº
Indications
  • Elderly patients with severe comminution
  • Unfixable articular surface
  • Periprosthetic fracture with loose TKA component
Key Points
  • Advantage: Immediate weight bearing
  • Tradeoff: Reduced implant longevity
  • Not ideal for young patients
When bone cannot hold screws or plates, replacement gives the patient immediate mobility.
07

Complications

Knee StiffnessMost Common
Most frequent overall complication. Related to soft tissue disruption and prolonged immobilization.
Varus CollapseMost Common Deformity
Due to adductor pull and failure of medial fixation. More common with inadequate plate length.
Nonunion
Up to ~18% with ORIF. Higher risk with comminution and bone loss.
Vascular InjuryEarly
Popliteal artery injury. ABI <0.9 warrants angiography. Delayed diagnosis ‚Üí limb loss.
Malunion
Most commonly varus. Affects gait and knee load distribution.
Implant Failure
More common with short plates, poor distal fixation, or early weight bearing.

What deformity does the gastrocnemius cause, and how would you recognize it on imaging?Tap to reveal
Apex-posterior (extension) deformity. On the lateral X-ray, the distal fragment tilts posteriorly relative to the shaft. Recognizing this helps you anticipate flexion needed to reduce the fragment intraoperatively.
What is a Hoffa fracture, and why is it commonly missed?Tap to reveal
A coronal plane fracture of a femoral condyle ‚Äî most commonly the lateral. Missed because it is not well visualized on AP radiographs. It must be identified on the lateral view and confirmed with CT. Any intra-articular extension ‚Üí get CT.
What is the correct starting point for a retrograde femoral nail?Tap to reveal
On AP view: center of the intercondylar notch. On lateral view: at Blumensaat's line. An incorrect start point is the most common cause of malreduction ‚Äî particularly sagittal plane recurvatum.
When would you choose distal femoral replacement over ORIF in an elderly patient?Tap to reveal
When bone cannot reliably hold internal fixation ‚Äî severe osteoporosis, highly comminuted articular fracture, or a periprosthetic fracture with a loose TKA component. Primary advantage is immediate weight bearing.
What ABI value should prompt angiography?Tap to reveal
ABI <0.9 should prompt angiography. The popliteal artery is tethered proximally and distally, making it vulnerable to traction and direct injury. Delay ‚Üí limb ischemia and amputation.
For an OTA-33C fracture, what is the correct order of fixation steps?Tap to reveal
Restore and fix the articular surface first ‚Äî anatomic reduction of the joint is the priority. Then reduce and fix the metaphysis/diaphysis to the reconstructed articular block. Fixing the shaft first compromises your ability to visualize and reduce the joint.
