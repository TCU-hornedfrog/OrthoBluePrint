Tibial Plateau Fracture

Intra-articular fractures involving one or both condyles of the proximal tibia. Tibial plateau fractures compromise knee stability, alignment, and articular congruity ‚Äî all of which must be restored for a good outcome.
Schatzker Classification Axial + Valgus Force Compartment Syndrome Risk

Key concept: Lateral plateau = valgus + axial load (most common). Medial plateau = high energy only. Always rule out compartment syndrome, popliteal artery injury, and peroneal nerve injury.
01

Epidemiology

Population 1
Young Males ‚Äî High Energy
MVA, pedestrian vs. vehicle, fall from height. Bicondylar and comminuted patterns. Associated ligamentous and neurovascular injuries are common.
Population 2
Elderly Females ‚Äî Low Energy
Fall from standing with axial and valgus force. Lateral plateau depression on osteoporotic bone. Schatzker I‚ÄìIII most common in this group.

Anatomy & Deforming Forces

Lateral plateau is convex and weaker ‚Äî fractured more often. Medial plateau is concave and stronger ‚Äî fracture implies high-energy injury. The posterior tibial slope (~10¬∞) must be restored to maintain normal knee kinematics.

Schatzker Classification ‚Äî Know This Cold

I
Lateral Split
Pure split of lateral plateau. Good bone, young patient. No depression.
II
Split-Depression
Lateral split with central depression. Most common overall pattern.
III
Pure Depression
Central depression only, no peripheral split. Elderly, osteoporotic bone.
IV
Medial Plateau
High energy. Popliteal artery and peroneal nerve at risk.
Vascular exam is mandatory with every Schatzker IV.
V
Bicondylar
Both plateaus fractured, metaphysis still attached to diaphysis.
VI
Bicondylar + Dissociation
Articular block fully separated from diaphysis. Highest energy, worst soft tissue injury.

Operative threshold: >3 mm articular depression or >5 mm condylar widening in most patients. Lower threshold in young, active patients.
04

Presentation & Must-Not-Miss

Painful, swollen knee following mechanism. Hemarthrosis is classic. Inability to weight bear. Always examine all four compartments of the leg ‚Äî anterior compartment syndrome is a real risk with high-energy bicondylar fractures.

üö®
Must not miss:
‚Ä¢ Compartment syndrome ‚Äî 5 Ps (pain, pressure, paresthesia, paralysis, pallor)
‚Ä¢ Popliteal artery injury ‚Äî check ABI bilaterally; ABI <0.9 ‚Üí CTA/angiography
‚Ä¢ Common peroneal nerve ‚Äî dorsiflexion and first web space sensation
05

Imaging

X-rays: AP + lateral of the knee. Obtain full-length tibia films to rule out ipsilateral shaft fracture. Look for a lipohemarthrosis on the lateral view ‚Äî fat-fluid level in the suprapatellar pouch confirms intra-articular fracture.

ü©ª
CT scan is mandatory for all tibial plateau fractures. Defines degree of depression, condylar split, and comminution. Essential for operative planning ‚Äî you cannot adequately plan without it.

Nonoperative

  • Non-ambulatory or prohibitive surgical risk
Hinged knee brace + non-weight bearing 6‚Äì12 weeks. Requires close radiographic follow-up to monitor for displacement.
07

Complications

Knee Stiffness Most Common
Related to prolonged immobilization and soft tissue trauma. Early ROM is critical after fixation.
Post-Traumatic Arthritis
Direct result of articular cartilage injury at the time of fracture. Risk increases with depression >3 mm or malreduction.
Compartment Syndrome Early
Anterior compartment most commonly affected. High-energy bicondylar fractures at highest risk. Emergent four-compartment fasciotomy required.
Vascular Injury Early
Popliteal artery most at risk with Schatzker IV (medial plateau). ABI <0.9 ‚Üí emergent angiography.
Wound Complications
Staged approach dramatically reduces wound breakdown risk in bicondylar fractures with poor soft tissue envelopes.
Malunion / Varus Collapse
Failure to address the posteromedial fragment in Schatzker V/VI is the most common cause of late varus deformity.

‚ùì Attending-Level Questions
What is the operative threshold for articular depression in a tibial plateau fracture? Tap to reveal
Generally >3 mm of articular depression or >5 mm of condylar widening ‚Äî but threshold is lower in young, active patients where any malalignment accelerates post-traumatic arthritis. Always factor in patient age, activity level, and bone quality.
Why do you stage high-energy bicondylar tibial plateau fractures rather than fix them acutely? Tap to reveal
Significant soft tissue swelling with fracture blisters makes early definitive ORIF high-risk for wound complications and deep infection. A spanning external fixator restores length and alignment while the soft tissue envelope recovers. Definitive ORIF is performed at 7‚Äì14 days once the "wrinkle sign" returns ‚Äî indicating soft tissue edema has resolved sufficiently.
Where is the posteromedial fragment and why does it matter so much in Schatzker V/VI? Tap to reveal
The posteromedial fragment is the posterior and medial portion of the tibial plateau. It cannot be visualized or fixed through a standard lateral approach. If not addressed with a separate posteromedial incision and plate, the construct will fail in varus ‚Äî the most common cause of malunion in bicondylar tibial plateau fractures.
What is a lipohemarthrosis and what does it tell you on X-ray? Tap to reveal
A fat-fluid level in the suprapatellar pouch seen on a cross-table lateral X-ray. Fat from ruptured marrow rises above the blood layer, creating a horizontal interface. Its presence confirms an intra-articular fracture even when the fracture line is subtle ‚Äî mandating CT imaging.
What nerve is at risk with a Schatzker IV medial plateau fracture and what is your exam? Tap to reveal
The common peroneal nerve wraps around the fibular neck and is at risk with high-energy medial plateau and knee dislocation patterns. Examine motor function (ankle dorsiflexion ‚Äî EHL, tibialis anterior) and sensation (first dorsal web space). Document this on every patient before and after reduction.
You fill the void beneath an elevated plateau fragment with bone graft ‚Äî why is this mandatory? Tap to reveal
After elevating a depressed fragment, a subchondral void remains. This void will not fill with bone ‚Äî it will collapse under weight bearing, re-depressing the articular surface. Bone graft or substitute (calcium phosphate, bone marrow aspirate + allograft) provides structural support to maintain reduction until healing occurs.
