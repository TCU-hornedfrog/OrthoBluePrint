Knee Dislocation

Complete disruption of the tibiofemoral articulation requiring injury to at least two major ligamentous complexes. A limb-threatening injury ‚Äî the popliteal artery is tethered and cannot escape. Up to 50% reduce spontaneously before presentation.
Vascular Emergency Multi-Ligament Injury ABI on Every Patient
üö®
This is a vascular emergency until proven otherwise. The popliteal artery is tethered at the adductor hiatus proximally and the anterior tibial takeoff distally ‚Äî it cannot escape. Obtain bilateral ABI on every patient. ABI <0.9 ‚Üí emergent CTA or angiography.
01

Epidemiology & Mechanism

High Energy
MVA / Contact Sports
Motor vehicle accidents, dashboard injuries, high-energy contact. Both cruciate ligaments typically disrupted. Full deformity often visible on arrival.
Low Energy (Ultra-Low)
Morbidly Obese Patients
BMI >40. Simple fall or misstep. Popliteal artery injury equally likely despite trivial mechanism. Do not underestimate these patients ‚Äî they are a distinct, high-risk population.
02

Anatomy ‚Äî Why Vascular Injury is Unavoidable

The popliteal artery is uniquely vulnerable because it is tethered at two points: proximally where it passes through the adductor hiatus, and distally where it divides into the anterior tibial artery and tibioperoneal trunk. Unlike the brachial artery in elbow dislocations, the popliteal artery cannot slip away from the deforming force ‚Äî it stretches, tears, or sustains an intimal injury.

05

Imaging

X-rays: AP + lateral to confirm reduction and identify associated fractures (Segond fracture, fibular head avulsion = PLC injury). MRI: After vascular clearance and stable reduction ‚Äî defines all ligamentous injuries for surgical planning. CTA: For ABI <0.9 or equivocal exam.

Treatment

01
Acute: Reduction + Vascular Management
Immediate priority ‚Äî within minutes to hours

Steps
  • Closed reduction under sedation ‚Äî longitudinal traction + correction of deformity
  • Splint at 20¬∞ flexion post-reduction

02
Multi-Ligament Reconstruction
Definitive surgical treatment ‚Äî timing is debated
‚ñº
Timing Principles
  • PLC injuries: Early repair within 2‚Äì3 weeks (heals poorly with delay)
  • Cruciates: Delayed reconstruction at 6‚Äì8 weeks reduces stiffness risk
  • Vascular repair takes absolute priority ‚Äî ligament reconstruction follows when safe
Surgical Priorities
  • Address all injured structures ‚Äî missing the PLC is the most common cause of reconstruction failure
  • PLC does not heal without surgery ‚Äî it must be reconstructed
The PLC must always be addressed. Reconstructing cruciates in the presence of a neglected PLC injury leads to early graft failure from uncorrected varus/rotational instability.
03
Nonoperative (Selected Cases)
KD-I, elderly low-demand patients, vascular repair cases
‚ñº
Candidates

  • Elderly, low-demand patients unwilling or unfit for extensive reconstruction
  • Patients who required vascular repair ‚Äî staged approach with ligamentous rehab first
Most knee dislocations in active patients require surgical reconstruction for functional stability. Nonoperative management reliably produces chronic instability.

Complications

Popliteal Artery Injury Limb-Threatening
5‚Äì40% incidence. Delayed diagnosis causes limb loss. Intimal tears present with normal pulse initially ‚Äî serial exams mandatory.
Peroneal Nerve Injury
20‚Äì40% incidence. Complete peroneal palsy has poor prognosis ‚Äî only 30‚Äì40% recovery. AFO for foot drop. Lateral and posterolateral dislocations at highest risk.
Knee Stiffness Most Common Long-Term
Arthrofibrosis after multi-ligament reconstruction. Aggressive early ROM protocol is essential. Goal: 90¬∞ by 6 weeks.
Missed PLC Injury ‚Üí Graft Failure
The most common cause of failed multi-ligament reconstruction. Unrecognized PLC injury causes varus/rotational instability that stretches out cruciates.
Post-Traumatic Arthritis
Cartilage damage at time of dislocation and chronic instability episodes accelerate degeneration.
DVT / Pulmonary Embolism
High-energy trauma, immobility, and vascular injury significantly increase VTE risk. Chemoprophylaxis when safe.

Pocket Pimped

‚ùì Attending-Level Questions
Why is the popliteal artery specifically at risk in knee dislocations compared to other joints? Tap to reveal
The popliteal artery is tethered at two anatomical fixed points ‚Äî proximally at the adductor hiatus (Hunter's canal) and distally at its bifurcation into the anterior tibial artery and tibioperoneal trunk. Unlike vessels at other joints that can slide away from deforming forces, the popliteal artery is constrained at both ends and must absorb the entire displacement force ‚Äî resulting in stretch injury, intimal tear, or complete rupture.
What is an irreducible knee dislocation and what is the pathoanatomy? Tap to reveal
A posterolateral rotatory dislocation in which the medial femoral condyle buttonholes through the posteromedial capsule, trapping it between the condyle and the tibia. The MCL acts as a noose around the condyle. Closed reduction is impossible and risks neurovascular injury. The dimple sign (skin puckering at the posteromedial knee) is pathognomonic. Treatment: open reduction through a medial approach.
A knee dislocation has been reduced, the pulse is present, and the ABI is 0.92. Are you done? Tap to reveal
No. An intimal tear can present initially with a palpable pulse and borderline ABI but progress to thrombosis and occlusion over 6‚Äì24 hours. Serial neurovascular exams every 2‚Äì4 hours for 24 hours are mandatory. Any deterioration ‚Äî new pain, paresthesias, change in pulse quality ‚Äî requires immediate vascular surgery consultation and emergent angiography.
Why must the PLC be addressed in multi-ligament reconstruction, and when should it be repaired vs. reconstructed? Tap to reveal
The posterolateral corner heals extremely poorly without surgery due to its complex three-dimensional anatomy and poor blood supply. Unaddressed PLC instability causes chronic varus and external rotation laxity that places excessive tensile load on any cruciate graft ‚Äî leading to early failure. Repair is possible and preferred within 2‚Äì3 weeks of injury (tissue quality still adequate). After 3 weeks, scarring and retraction require reconstruction (LaPrade fibular-based technique).
What is the ischemia window for popliteal artery repair after knee dislocation? Tap to reveal
6‚Äì8 hours of warm ischemia time. Beyond this window, irreversible muscle and nerve ischemia occurs in the leg and foot. After 8 hours, amputation rates rise dramatically. Skeletal stabilization (external fixator) is performed simultaneously with or just after vascular repair to prevent re-injury to the reconstructed artery.
What is the Schenck KD classification and what does KD-IIIL mean? Tap to reveal
Schenck's classification grades knee dislocations by ligament injury. KD-I: one cruciate intact. KD-II: both cruciates, collaterals intact. KD-IIIM: both cruciates + MCL. KD-IIIL: both cruciates + PLC (lateral structures). KD-IV: all four major ligaments. KD-V: fracture-dislocation. KD-IIIL (PLC involvement) carries the worst prognosis for peroneal nerve injury and chronic instability if the PLC is missed.
