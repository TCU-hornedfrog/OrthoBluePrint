Total Knee Arthroplasty

Surgical resurfacing of the distal femur, proximal tibia, and patella with prosthetic components to restore painless, functional knee motion in end-stage arthritis. One of the most successful elective operations in all of medicine.
~750,000 Per Year (US) Neutral Mechanical Axis Gap Balancing is Everything

Key concept for the wards: The two goals of TKA are a neutral mechanical axis and balanced, equal flexion and extension gaps. If you understand these principles, you can follow any TKA conversation on rounds.

Surgical Technique & Gap Balancing

Mechanical axis: A line from the center of the femoral head through the center of the knee to the center of the ankle. The goal is neutral (180¬∞) ‚Äî equal loading across both compartments. Malalignment accelerates PE wear and causes early loosening.

Technical PrincipleClinical Significance
Extension GapSpace between distal femoral cut and proximal tibial cut at full extension. Must equal flexion gap.
Flexion GapSpace at 90¬∞ flexion. Must equal extension gap for stability throughout ROM.
Femoral RotationReferenced to trans-epicondylar axis (TEA). Malrotation causes patellar maltracking and anterior knee pain.
Tibial Slope (3‚Äì7¬∞)Affects flexion and PCL tension. Excessive slope ‚Üí instability in extension.
Patellar Tracking ("No Thumb" Test)Patella should track centrally in the groove without holding it (thumb) after closure.
üí°
Gap balancing simplified:
Tight in flexion ‚Üí upsize femoral component or increase tibial slope
Tight in extension ‚Üí resect more distal femur or release posterior capsule
Flexion gap > extension gap ‚Üí downsize femoral component or reduce tibial slope
04

Complications

Stiffness / Arthrofibrosis Most Common Early
Goal is ‚â•90¬∞ flexion by 6 weeks. If inadequate ROM at 6‚Äì12 weeks despite PT, manipulation under anesthesia (MUA) is indicated before scar matures.
Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)
1‚Äì2% incidence. Most devastating complication. Early acute (<3‚Äì4 weeks): DAIR (debridement, antibiotics, irrigation, retention). Late/chronic: 2-stage revision (explant + spacer ‚Üí reimplant).
Aseptic Loosening Most Common Late
PE wear generates debris ‚Üí osteolysis ‚Üí component migration. Most common cause of revision at 10‚Äì15 years. Improved PE formulations (highly cross-linked) have reduced this.
Instability
Flexion or extension instability from gap imbalance, ligament injury, or component malposition. Most common indication for early revision after PJI.
Patellar Complications
Maltracking (femoral rotational error most common), patellar fracture (stress riser at peg holes), patellar clunk (PS design ‚Äî fibrous nodule in intercondylar box).
Periprosthetic Fracture
Distal femur most common. ORIF if implant stable and well-fixed. Revision arthroplasty if implant loose. Anterior femoral notching during TKA increases fracture risk.
05

Outcomes & Revision

TKA has ~750,000 procedures/year in the US with a 15-year implant survival of 85‚Äì95%. Patient satisfaction at 1 year: approximately 85‚Äì90%. The most common causes of revision in order: aseptic loosening ‚Üí infection ‚Üí instability ‚Üí stiffness ‚Üí patellar complications. Revision TKA carries significantly higher complication rates than primary TKA.

Young, active patients (<55) and obese patients (BMI >40) have substantially higher revision rates. These are not contraindications, but they must be counselled accordingly ‚Äî TKA in a 45-year-old will likely require revision in their lifetime.

‚ùì Attending-Level Questions
Explain flexion and extension gap balancing. What happens if the flexion gap is larger than the extension gap? Tap to reveal
The extension gap is the space between the distal femoral cut and the tibial cut with the knee at full extension. The flexion gap is the same space at 90¬∞ flexion. Equal, symmetric gaps produce a stable, well-balanced knee. If the flexion gap is larger than the extension gap, the knee is loose in flexion (posterior instability at 90¬∞) ‚Äî correct by upsizing the femoral component, reducing tibial slope, or tightening the posterior capsule.
What is the difference between CR and PS TKA designs? When would you choose PS? Tap to reveal
CR (cruciate-retaining): PCL is preserved, maintained for posterior rollback. Requires an intact, functional PCL. PS (posterior-stabilized): PCL is sacrificed; a cam-post mechanism substitutes for PCL function and drives femoral rollback. PS is preferred when: significant deformity requiring extensive soft tissue release (including PCL), PCL-deficient knee, inflammatory arthritis, or revision surgery. Long-term outcomes are comparable between the two designs.
What is the 2-stage revision protocol for periprosthetic joint infection? Tap to reveal
Stage 1: Remove all components, thorough d√©bridement of infected tissue, culture-directed antibiotic-loaded cement spacer (articulating or static), and 6 weeks of IV antibiotics based on organism. Stage 2: After confirmed eradication (negative ESR/CRP, negative joint aspiration), reimplant new components. DAIR (d√©bridement, antibiotics, implant retention) is appropriate only for acute early infections (<3‚Äì4 weeks) with a stable, well-fixed implant and susceptible organism.
What causes anterior knee pain after TKA and how does femoral component rotation factor in? Tap to reveal
Anterior knee pain after TKA is most commonly due to patellar maltracking, which is usually caused by internal rotation of the femoral component. The femoral component should be rotated to the trans-epicondylar axis (TEA). Internal rotation causes the trochlear groove to shift medially, lateralizing the patella and increasing lateral facet contact pressure. The "no thumb" test intraoperatively ‚Äî patella tracks centrally without lateral support ‚Äî confirms correct rotational alignment.
What is patellar clunk syndrome and in which TKA design does it occur? Tap to reveal
Patellar clunk occurs in posterior-stabilized (PS) TKA designs. A fibrous nodule forms at the proximal pole of the patellar button at the attachment of the quadriceps tendon. As the knee extends from ~60¬∞ to 30¬∞, this nodule catches on the anterior edge of the intercondylar cam box ‚Äî creating a palpable, often audible clunk. Treatment is arthroscopic excision of the fibrous nodule. Seen less commonly with modern PS femoral component designs that have a more open box geometry.
What is anterior femoral notching and why does it matter? Tap to reveal
Anterior femoral notching occurs when the anterior femoral cutting guide removes too much bone from the anterior femoral cortex ‚Äî creating a stress riser. This increases the risk of supracondylar periprosthetic fracture, particularly in osteoporotic patients. Notching of >3 mm significantly elevates fracture risk. Prevention: careful sizing and positioning of the femoral component to avoid violating the anterior cortex.
